## 1. Eisenstein Integers: The Third Root of Unity

### 1.1 Definition

The Eisenstein integers are the lattice:

$$\mathbb{Z}[\omega] = \{a + b\omega : a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}$$

where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3} = -\frac{1}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i$ is a primitive third root of unity.

### 1.2 Why 3?

The $n$-th roots of unity $\{e^{2\pi ik/n} : k = 0, \ldots, n-1\}$ form vertices of a regular $n$-gon. For the lattice generated by $1$ and $e^{2\pi i/n}$ to be discrete in $\mathbb{R}^2$:

- **n = 2:** $1$ and $-1$ generate $\mathbb{Z}$ — a 1D lattice (line). No area. No plane.
- **n = 3:** $1$ and $\omega$ generate $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ — a 2D hexagonal lattice. **This is the first non-trivial lattice in the plane.**
- **n = 4:** $1$ and $i$ generate the Gaussian integers $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ — a square lattice. Valid, but not minimal.
- **n = 6:** $1$ and $e^{\pi i/3}$ generate $\mathbb{Z}[e^{\pi i/3}]$ — which equals $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ (since $\omega = -e^{\pi i/3}$). Same lattice.

**Theorem.** *The smallest $n$ for which $\{1, e^{2\pi i/n}\}$ generates a discrete lattice in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (rather than $\mathbb{R}^1$) is $n = 3$.*

*Proof.* For $n = 2$, the set $\{1, -1\}$ is collinear — the lattice is $\mathbb{Z} \subset \mathbb{R}^1$. For $n = 3$, the angle between $1$ and $\omega$ is $120°$, which is not $0°$ or $180°$, so the two generators are linearly independent over $\mathbb{R}$. By standard lattice theory, two $\mathbb{R}$-independent generators in $\mathbb{R}^2$ produce a discrete subgroup of rank 2. ∎

### 1.3 Why the Hexagonal Lattice is Optimal

The hexagonal lattice $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ is the unique lattice that solves two optimization problems simultaneously:

1. **Sphere packing in 2D** (Thue's theorem, 1892): The hexagonal packing is the densest possible circle packing in the plane.
2. **Lattice quantizer in 2D**: The hexagonal lattice minimizes the mean squared quantization error per unit area (Fechner-Navarro, 1960s).

Both results hold because the Voronoi cell of the hexagonal lattice is a regular hexagon — the unique polygon that tiles the plane with **maximum minimum distance** between lattice points. This is a direct consequence of 3-fold symmetry: the equilateral triangle is the most "spread out" triangle (maximizes the minimum edge length for a given circumradius), and the hexagonal lattice is the tiling by equilateral triangles.

### 1.4 Connection to Constraint Theory

Our constraint theory uses $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ as the weight lattice for SplineLinear layers. The choice is not aesthetic — it is **geometrically optimal**:

- **Hexagonal weight grid** → maximum inter-weight spacing → best generalization for a given parameter budget.
- **3-fold rotational symmetry** → the weight space has no preferred direction, which means the model treats all input directions equally (rotation-equivariant feature extraction).
- **The hexagonal lattice is self-dual** → the Fourier transform of the weight lattice maps to itself, which means convolution in weight space is closed. This is why our spline parameterization is algebraically clean.

## 2. Three Observations Define Periodicity

### 2.1 The Pulsar Problem

A pulsar emits periodic radio pulses. How many pulses do you need to confirm the signal is periodic (not random noise)?

**The answer is 3.** Here's why.

### 2.2 Estimating the Period

Given observation times $t_1 < t_2 < t_3$:

$$T \approx \frac{t_3 - t_1}{2}$$

With 2 observations, you have one interval $t_2 - t_1$. This could be anything — a single random event. You cannot distinguish period $T$ from period $2T$, from noise.

With 3 observations, you have **two independent intervals** $(t_2 - t_1, t_3 - t_2)$. If they agree (within tolerance), you have evidence of periodicity. If they don't, you know it's not a simple period.

### 2.3 Statistical Confirmation

To test $H_0$: "observations are random" vs $H_1$: "observations are periodic", you need enough degrees of freedom to construct a test statistic.

A chi-squared goodness-of-fit test requires at least 3 bins (and therefore ≥3 observations) to have $df \geq 1$:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i}, \quad df = k - 1 \geq 1 \implies k \geq 2$$

But with $k = 2$ bins, the test has essentially no power. You need $k \geq 3$ (hence $df \geq 2$) for a meaningful test. This means **3 observations is the practical minimum**.

### 2.4 Fundamental vs Harmonics

With 3 evenly-spaced observations, you can distinguish the fundamental frequency from its first harmonic:

- If observations are consistent with period $T$, then $(t_2 - t_1) \approx (t_3 - t_2) \approx T$.
- If observations are consistent with period $2T$ (fundamental), the same intervals are $\approx T$ each.
- But with only 2 observations, you can't tell if the period is $T$ or $2T$ or any multiple.

Three observations give you two intervals. Two intervals let you estimate the period AND estimate the uncertainty. One interval gives you neither.

### 2.5 The Ocean Swell Analogy

Ocean waves: you need 3 wave crests to:
1. Estimate the swell period ($T \approx \Delta t_{\text{crest-to-crest}}$).
2. Confirm it's a swell (coherent, long-period) vs wind chop (random, short-period).
3. Separate the swell from the local wind sea using the directional spectrum.

The "3-crest rule" is taught in surf forecasting and naval architecture for the same reason pulsar astronomers use 3 pulses: **structure begins at 3**.

## 3. Three-Phase Power: Constant Transfer at 120°

### 3.1 The Setup

Three sinusoidal voltages, phase-shifted by 120°:

$$V_1(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t)$$
$$V_2(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t + 120°)$$
$$V_3(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t + 240°)$$

### 3.2 The Magic Identity

**Theorem.** *The instantaneous power delivered by a balanced three-phase system into a balanced resistive load is constant (time-independent).*

*Proof.* The total instantaneous power is:

$$P(t) = V_1^2(t)/R + V_2^2(t)/R + V_3^2(t)/R$$

$$= \frac{V_0^2}{R}\left[\sin^2(\omega t) + \sin^2(\omega t + 2\pi/3) + \sin^2(\omega t + 4\pi/3)\right]$$

Using the identity $\sin^2\theta = \frac{1}{2}(1 - \cos 2\theta)$:

$$= \frac{V_0^2}{R}\left[\frac{3}{2} - \frac{1}{2}\left(\cos 2\omega t + \cos(2\omega t + 4\pi/3) + \cos(2\omega t + 8\pi/3)\right)\right]$$

The sum $\cos(2\omega t) + \cos(2\omega t + 4\pi/3) + \cos(2\omega t + 8\pi/3) = 0$ because this is the sum of three vectors at $120°$ apart in the complex plane (geometric series with ratio $e^{i4\pi/3}$, sum = $\frac{1 - e^{i4\pi}}{1 - e^{i4\pi/3}} = 0$).

Therefore: $P(t) = \frac{3V_0^2}{2R} = \text{const}$. ∎

### 3.3 Why Not 2 Phases?

Two-phase power at 180° apart:

$$V_1(t) + V_2(t) = V_0\sin(\omega t) + V_0\sin(\omega t + \pi) = V_0\sin(\omega t) - V_0\sin(\omega t) = 0$$

The voltages cancel. The power is $P(t) = \frac{2V_0^2}{R}\sin^2(\omega t)$, which pulsates at twice the line frequency. No constant transfer.

Two-phase at 90° (quadrature):

$$P(t) = \frac{V_0^2}{R}[\sin^2(\omega t) + \cos^2(\omega t)] = \frac{V_0^2}{R} = \text{const}$$

This works! But the return conductor carries $\sqrt{2}V_0$ (the vector sum), requiring heavier wire. Three-phase has the neutral current be exactly **zero** in a balanced system, saving a conductor. Three phases is the **minimum for zero-return-current constant power**.

### 3.4 The Crankshaft Connection

A 3-cylinder engine with crank throws at 120° has:

- **Self-balancing first-order forces**: The three reciprocating masses produce forces at 120° apart that sum to zero (same vector identity as above).
- **Self-balancing first-order moments**: The three moments also sum to zero when the cylinder spacing is symmetric.
- **Constant torque delivery**: The torque waveform from each cylinder is a pulse. Three pulses at 120° spacing overlap to give nearly constant net torque.

This is not a coincidence. The engine and the power grid use the same geometry because they face the same problem: **how to produce smooth output from periodic inputs.** The answer is "three sources at 120°" because that's the minimum for complete cancellation of the first harmonic.

### 3.5 Connection to Eisenstein

The three phase vectors $1$, $\omega$, $\omega^2$ (where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$) are exactly the three Eisenstein units. The power identity $V_1 + V_2 + V_3 = 0$ is the statement that $\{1, \omega, \omega^2\}$ sum to zero, which holds because:

$$1 + \omega + \omega^2 = \frac{\omega^3 - 1}{\omega - 1} = \frac{1 - 1}{\omega - 1} = 0$$

The cyclotomic equation strikes again. The same algebra that makes Eisenstein integers a lattice makes three-phase power smooth.

## 4. Laman Rigidity: Three Edges Make a Triangle

### 4.1 Laman's Theorem (1970)

A graph $G = (V, E)$ with $n$ vertices in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is **generically rigid** if and only if:

1. $|E| = 2n - 3$
2. For every subgraph on $n'$ vertices: $|E'| \leq 2n' - 3$

### 4.2 The Triangle: Minimally Rigid Graph

For $n = 3$ vertices:
- Condition 1: $|E| = 2(3) - 3 = 3$ edges needed.
- Condition 2: Every subgraph with $n' = 2$ vertices has at most $2(2) - 3 = 1$ edge (trivially satisfied).

A triangle with 3 edges on 3 vertices is the **smallest rigid graph**.

With 2 edges on 3 vertices: you have a path graph. This is a **hinge** — the two edges can rotate freely around the middle vertex. Not rigid.

### 4.3 Why $2n - 3$?

A rigid body in 2D has 3 degrees of freedom: translation in $x$, translation in $y$, and rotation $\theta$. So $n$ free vertices have $2n$ DOF. To be rigid, you need to eliminate all but 3 DOF (the rigid body motions). That requires $2n - 3$ constraints (edges).

**The 3 residual DOF correspond to the 3 rigid body motions: $x$, $y$, $\theta$.** You always leave 3 DOF on the table because the structure must be free to move as a rigid body. Three is not arbitrary — it's the dimension of the Euclidean group $SE(2)$.

### 4.4 Connection to Eisenstein

The hexagonal lattice tiles the plane with **equilateral triangles**. Each equilateral triangle is a Laman-rigid graph. So the hexagonal lattice is a tiling of minimally rigid structures. This is why it's the natural home for constraint theory:

- **Constraint satisfaction** = rigidity in some configuration space.
- **Minimally rigid** = exactly the right number of constraints (no redundancy, no underdetermination).
- **The triangle is the building block** of rigidity, and the hexagonal lattice is the triangle's natural tiling.

### 4.5 The Deep Connection

| Concept | Minimum for Structure | Why |
|---------|----------------------|-----|
| Lattice in $\mathbb{R}^2$ | 3-fold symmetry (Eisenstein) | 2-fold gives line; 3-fold gives plane |
| Periodicity detection | 3 observations | 2 intervals needed to estimate period + uncertainty |
| Constant power transfer | 3 phases at 120° | Minimum for zero pulsation + zero return current |
| Rigidity in $\mathbb{R}^2$ | 3 edges (triangle) | $2n - 3$ gives 3 for $n = 3$; 2 gives hinge |
| Rigid body DOF in 2D | 3 ($x$, $y$, $\theta$) | Dimension of $SE(2)$ |

All trace back to the same fact: **$\mathbb{R}^2$ has dimension 2, and the Euclidean group $SE(2)$ has dimension 3.** To fix a structure in the plane, you must overcome all 3 DOF.

## 5. Transfer Entropy: Three Variables, Three Times

### 5.1 Definition

Transfer entropy from $X$ to $Y$ at lag $\tau$:

$$TE_{X \to Y}(\tau) = I(X_{t-\tau} ; Y_t \mid Y_{t-\tau})$$
$$= H(Y_t \mid Y_{t-\tau}) - H(Y_t \mid X_{t-\tau}, Y_{t-\tau})$$

where $I(\cdot;\cdot|\cdot)$ is conditional mutual information and $H(\cdot|\cdot)$ is conditional entropy.

### 5.2 Why Three?

The formula involves **three random variables**: $X_{t-\tau}$, $Y_{t-\tau}$, $Y_t$.

- With only $Y_t$ and $Y_{t-\tau}$ (2 variables): you can compute the autocorrelation of $Y$, but you cannot distinguish $Y$'s own dynamics from external influence.
- With $X_{t-\tau}$ and $Y_t$ (2 variables, no conditioning): you can compute $I(X_{t-\tau}; Y_t)$, but this conflates $X$'s influence with $Y$'s own memory (confounding).
- With all three ($X_{t-\tau}$, $Y_{t-\tau}$, $Y_t$): you condition on $Y$'s own history ($Y_{t-\tau}$) to isolate $X$'s **unique** contribution to predicting $Y$.

### 5.3 The Causal Interpretation

Transfer entropy is not causality, but it is the **minimum information-theoretic quantity that can detect directed information flow**:

1. $I(X;Y)$ — symmetric, no direction.
2. $I(X;Y|Z)$ — asymmetric in $X$ vs $Y$ when $Z$ is the history of $Y$. **Direction emerges.**
3. You need exactly 3 variables for this asymmetry: source, target, and the target's own past.

Two variables give you correlation. Three give you **directed influence**.

### 5.4 Connection to the Signal Chain

Our signal chain (filter → model → validate) mirrors transfer entropy's structure:

| Stage | Role | Analog in TE |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Filter (rule-based) | Remove known patterns | $Y_{t-\tau}$ — condition on known history |
| Model (ML) | Detect novel patterns | $I(X_{t-\tau}; Y_t \mid \cdot)$ — unique contribution |
| Validate (deadband) | Confirm significance | Compare $TE$ to null distribution |

The model is sandwiched between two code stages for the same reason $X_{t-\tau}$ is sandwiched between $Y_{t-\tau}$ and $Y_t$: **you must control for the known before detecting the new.**

## 6. CRDTs: Three Properties, Three Failures

### 6.1 Definition

A Conflict-Free Replicated Data Type (CRDT) requires three algebraic properties:

1. **Commutativity**: $f(a, b) = f(b, a)$ — order doesn't matter.
2. **Associativity**: $f(f(a,b), c) = f(a, f(b,c))$ — grouping doesn't matter.
3. **Idempotence**: $f(a, a) = a$ — duplicates don't matter.

### 6.2 What Breaks Without Each

| Missing property | Failure mode | Fleet analog |
|-----------------|-------------|-------------|
| Commutativity | Agent A's update at $t_1$ conflicts with Agent B's update at $t_2$; result depends on delivery order | Oracle1 and Forgemaster both update the same PLATO room; result depends on who writes last |
| Associativity | Merging 3 updates in different groupings gives different results | 3 agents merge I2I tiles; $(A \oplus B) \oplus C \neq A \oplus (B \oplus C)$ |
| Idempotence | Retry/duplicate delivery creates spurious state | Heartbeat retries cause duplicate task entries |

### 6.3 Why Exactly Three?

Each property addresses a **different failure mode of distributed systems**:

1. **Commutativity** solves **message reordering** — the network doesn't guarantee delivery order.
2. **Associativity** solves **partition merging** — when subgroups operate independently and reconcile.
3. **Idempotence** solves **duplicate delivery** — the network doesn't guarantee exactly-once semantics.

These are the three fundamental failure modes of asynchronous networks (proven in distributed systems theory: Fischer-Lynch-Paterson, 1985, and the CAP theorem). You need exactly one algebraic property per failure mode. No more, no fewer.

### 6.4 Connection to Laman

A CRDT is a **rigid structure in the space of possible orderings**. Without all three properties, the data structure has "hinge points" — configurations where different delivery orders lead to different results. With all three, the structure is rigid: any delivery order converges to the same state.

This is exactly analogous to Laman rigidity:
- 2 edges on 3 vertices = hinge (not rigid) = CRDT missing one property.
- 3 edges on 3 vertices = triangle (rigid) = CRDT with all three properties.

The triangle of commutativity, associativity, and idempotence makes the distributed state **Laman-rigid**.

## 7. The Signal Chain: Three Stages

### 7.1 The Architecture

```
Input → [FILTER] → [MODEL] → [VALIDATE] → Output
         (rules)    (ML)      (deadband)
         code       learned   code
```

### 7.2 Why Three Stages?

1. **Filter (code)**: Remove what we already understand. This is the prior — rule-based, deterministic, fast.
2. **Model (learned)**: Capture what we don't have rules for. This is the novelty detector — statistical, adaptive, slower.
3. **Validate (code)**: Confirm the model's output is real. This is the guard — prevents hallucination, enforces deadband, rejects noise.

### 7.3 The Sandwich Principle

The model is sandwiched between two code stages for the same reason transfer entropy conditions on $Y_{t-\tau}$ before computing $I(X_{t-\tau}; Y_t)$:

- **Pre-filter** removes the known → the model only sees the residual (novel signal).
- **Post-validate** checks the model's output → prevents the model from inventing structure.

Without the pre-filter, the model wastes capacity re-learning known patterns. Without the post-validate, the model can hallucinate. **Both code stages are load-bearing.** The model is the filling; the filters are the bread.

### 7.4 Connection to Three-Phase

Like three-phase power, the signal chain has three stages that together produce **constant, reliable output**:
- Filter alone: misses novel patterns (like single-phase pulsating power).
- Model alone: hallucinates (like unconstrained optimization).
- Validate alone: has nothing to validate (like a circuit with no source).
- All three together: smooth, reliable detection (like balanced three-phase → constant power).

## Appendix A: Proof that $1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0$

Let $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$. Then $\omega^3 = 1$, so $\omega$ is a root of $x^3 - 1 = (x-1)(x^2 + x + 1)$.

Since $\omega \neq 1$, we have $\omega^2 + \omega + 1 = 0$.

Therefore: $1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0$. ∎

This single identity underpins:
- Eisenstein lattice structure
- Three-phase voltage cancellation ($V_1 + V_2 + V_3 = 0$)

Each vertex in $\mathbb{R}^2$ has 2 DOF. $n$ vertices have $2n$ DOF. A rigid body in 2D has 3 DOF ($SE(2)$). To pin down a rigid framework:

For a triangle ($n = 3$): $2(3) - 3 = 3$ edges. ∎

## Appendix C: Proof that Three-Phase Gives Zero Return Current

In a balanced three-phase system:

$$I_{\text{neutral}} = I_1 + I_2 + I_3 = I_0[\sin(\omega t) + \sin(\omega t + 2\pi/3) + \sin(\omega t + 4\pi/3)]$$

By the geometric series identity:

$$= I_0 \cdot \text{Im}\left[\sum_{k=0}^{2} e^{i(\omega t + 2\pi k/3)}\right] = I_0 \cdot \text{Im}\left[e^{i\omega t} \cdot \frac{1 - e^{i2\pi}}{1 - e^{i2\pi/3}}\right] = I_0 \cdot \text{Im}[0] = 0$$

since $e^{i2\pi} = 1$ and the numerator vanishes. ∎
